Flute

The Flute is a woodwind instrument, even though it's body is made of metal.
The assembly is very easy for this instrument. Some video's will be provided on the assembly and scales.
You want to try to hold the Flute at a 90 degree angle the best you can.
Some band directors get very picky about the angle your holding your Flute.
The Flute is small and light weight, so it is really easy to hold.
Flutes usually have melody, as their range is very high.
You must have good wind support and good posture for the higher notes.

Clarinet

The Clarinet is also light weight. You want to hold this instrument down, at a slight angle, in between your legs.
The Clarinet cannot rest on your leg, or the chair.
Clarinets usually have harmony and melody since they can reach two octaves.
But keep in mind, you also have to have good wind support and good posture to get a good sonding high note.
In my experiences in band, the clarinets who take on the challenge of reaching the higher octaves don't sound that good.
Some signs that your high octave notes aren't sounding good is that the notes will sound windy.
This is either because of wind support, or a damaged key.
Also, Clarinets can easily squeak, and it does not sound pretty.
This is also the embarrassing moments at concerts for beginners and even some of the higher classes.

Trombone

Trombones are a bit heavier then most of the instruments.
This instrument is the bass of most music.
You will hear some of the low brass complain that they hardly every get melody.
But when they do, it sounds amazing.
Trombones can also reach two octaves, but this is completely different from most og the instruments.
Trombones have a slide instead of keys.
With this in mind, you'll learn the different slide postitions.
The slide posititons go from 1-7.
If you do not match up with a position, you'll hear a weird note.
But, the downside is that if you have fast tempo songs, or eighth notes, be prepared to move your arm quickly.
This is usually where the weird notes come out.
You want to be decent at the slide positions and have it memorized so you can remember how far to go.
The Trombone rests on your shoulder, so over the years, it will create a small arc in the metal.
This maybe could affect your sound.
And also, this instrument does not have a reed.
You instead have a metal mouthpiece.

Alto Saxophone

The Alto Saxophone is easier to hold then you think.
The Saxophone can rest at your side, or like the clarinet.
Band directors prefer you to hold it like a clarinet, but I find it easier to hold off to the side.
The Alto Saxophone can reach two octaves as well, technically 3!
The note memorization is really easy for this instrument.
Alto's usually have harmony.
In certain songs, they will get melody for sadly only a few meassures.
Also, Alto Saxophones are common in Jazz music.
Though this instrument requires many steps to put it together, it is one of the most easiest to learn.
Sometimes Alto's will sometimes even hit some base notes depending on the song.
But, they are constantly out of tune.
You can tell this by using a tuner or tuner app and playing an F-Sharp with the octave key.
This will read either flat or sharp, and it's really difficult to make it a perfect tune.
Sometimes the mouthpiece will be pushed on the cork too far.
This will read as Sharp.
If the mouthpiece is not on far enough, then it will raad flat.
Posture and wind support are also needed to make a good sound.
I personally have been picked on a lot by my band director due to my Saxophone being Sharp.

Trumpet

The trumpet is... a difficult instrument to learn. Instead of keys or a slide, you have 3 valves that play different notes based on the fingerings.
And the trumpets are usually the loudest in the band, and they won't quiet down most of the time.
Band directors get very picky on how you hold your trumpet.
You have to hold it up at an angle, and you have to push the buttons with your finger tips.
